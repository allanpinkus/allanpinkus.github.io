Guttmann Family


    1. Introduction:

    This is a story about some of the Guttmann family. We start with Isaac Guttmann and Katherine Leinkauf Guttmann. But then mainly follow the line of Hermann Eisner Guttmann and Alfred Eisner Guttmann. We tell a bit about the firm Guttmann and Frank. And then add some comments and references with regards to the other parts of the family. Many details of the story remain obscure. As with most family histories reconstructed from surviving documents, the record is incomplete and often raises as many questions as it answers. But we were surprised by how much we did uncover and discover. Please feel free to contact us if you can add to or correct anything herein.

    2. Isaac and Katherine:

    Isaac Guttmann was born in Oct 1840 in the town of Reca which is near Senec. Senec is about 30 km east of Pressburg. Pressburg was the German name for the city of Bratislava, Slovakia. This entire area was at the time in the Hungarian part of the Austro-Hungarian Empire. The Jews of this area spoke German, and perhaps also Hungarian. Isaac was the son of Moses Guttmann (abt 1790 – 1874 Rete, Slovakia) and Miriam Regner Guttmann (1810 – Feb 10, 1900 Rete, Slovakia). Isaac first married Regina Blau (1843 – 1887) on 8 Aug 1862 in Dunaszerdahely (about 50 km southeast of Pressburg), see here. Regina and Isaac had two daughters. Julie was born on 25 Aug 1866 and Hermine (Hani) was born on 26 Nov 1869. Both were probably born in Dunaszerdahely. Isaac and Regina's families seem to have been traditional Orthodox Jews. The family story was that Regina died young, leading Isaac to remarry. While it is true that Regina died while fairly young, that is aged 44, (see here) the fact is that Isaac and Regina divorced. This is noted both on Isaac’s second marriage record (see here) and on Regina’s death record. It was a family secret. We do not know the reason for the divorce. Perhaps she had a mental breakdown or terrible postnatal depression. We can only speculate.

    Katherine (Katalin) Leinkauf was born in 1836 in Pressburg. Katherine was, from we can determine, one of six children of Hermann Leinkauf and Bella Weiss (born about 1796 in Floss, Germany – 9 Dec 1852, Pressburg). Four of Katherine's siblings emigrated from Pressburg to Mobile, Alabama, over some years in the mid 19th century. They had an uncle Abraham Weiss who had previously emigrated from Floss to Mobile. Katherine and a sister named Minna remained in Pressburg. On 12 June 1864 Katherine married Max Eisner in Ganserndof, which is also near Pressburg, (see here). Max Eisner was born about 1828 in Pressburg. We have no details as to when he died, but it was before 1874. Katherine and Max had two sons, namely Hermann, born 21 Mar 1865 in Vienna, and Alfred, born 19 Feb 1866, also in Vienna.

    In May of 1874, in Pressburg, the widow Katherine Leinkauf Eisner married the divorced Isaac Guttmann, (see here). The marriage was conducted at the Neolog (or conservative) synagogue in Pressburg. Marrying in a Neolog synagogue seems a significant break from Orthodox tradition. But this was becoming more and more common. Isaac officially adopted Katherine's two sons, and thus their family name became Guttmann. Katherine and Isaac then had two additional daughters, namely Berta born on 7 Apr 1875 in Pressburg, and Malvina born on 7 Oct 1876 also in Pressburg. Katherine and Isaac raised all 6 children, i.e., Katherine's two boys with Max Eisner, Isaac's two daughters with Regina Blau, and their two joint daughters.

    Isaac and Katalin moved to Novi Sad, in the province of Vojvodina in what is today Serbia where Isaac acquired large land holdings. They owned a large working quarry at Krcedin, about 40km from Novi Sad, where the land included fields, meadows, vineyards and buildings. They also owned a salt mine just outside of Budapest. More about this in Section ??. By the time Isaac died in 1906, they were living in Váci Street, a prime location in the middle of Budapest, close to the Danube river. Isaac headed the firm I. Guttmann and Sons. What exactly this was is unclear.

    Isaac died in Budapest on 4 Jan 1908 at the age of 65, (see here). An announcement of his death appeared in Pester Lloyd a German-language online daily newspaper from Budapest, (see here and here). Katherine died of a stroke on 22 Mar 1915 in Budapest at the age of 79, (see here). Katherine and Isaac are buried in the Kozma Street Jewish cemetery in Budapest. In 2025 the grave was restored by descendants, namely by Michelle Dyson and her family.


    2. Hermann and his children:

    As was stated above, Hermann was born Hermann Eisner on 21 Mar 1865 in Vienna. He and his brother Alfred were adopted by Isaac Guttmann and took the family name of Guttmann. Here and here are the adoption papers. We could not decipher a date on these papers. We assume it was about 1874. In 1895 Hermann married Margit Ungar. Here is their wedding certificate. We were only able to decipher the year. Margit was born on 17 Oct 1876 in Gyékényes, Hungary. She was the daughter of Samuel Ungar and Paula Kremsier Ungar. Hermann's main business seems to have centered in Novi Sad and environs. We will relate more on that later. But he and Margit were often to be found in Budapest. In fact these two cities were only about 300 km from each other. Hermann and Margit had 4 children, all of whom were born in Budapest. They were Georgina (Gyorgyi) born 20 Aug 1899, Luiza (Lutzi) born about 1902, Rudolf (Rudi) born 26 Mar 1905 and Veronia (Vera) born 4 Dec 1910. We have here a copy of Hermann's Yugoslavian citizenship certificate from 1934. His height is listed as 1.68 m, his face is oval in shape, and his hair is grey. In 1939 Hermann and Margit lived at Ulica Petra Zrinskog 2 in Novi Sad (today called Ulica Petra Drapsina). Hermann died on 4 Nov 1939 at the age of 74. The cause of death is listed as arteriosclerosis cardiac paralysis. Hermann is buried in the Novi Sad Jewish Cemetery, (see here). Margit survived the war as, surprisingly, did all four of her children. Margit was for a while in Budapest, in hiding, with her grandson Peter (Rudi's son), Peter's mother Anna, and Anna's Bela and Ela Herzl. After the war Margit returned to Novi Sad and was with Rudi and family. In December 1948 they emigrated to Israel. She then lived with her daughter ??? in Gedera, Israel. She died there in 1964 at the age of 88.

    Georgina (Gyorgi) was born on 20 Aug 1899 in Budapest. She was the eldest daughter of Hermann and Margit. On 30 Apr 1918 she married Dr. Armin (Hermann) Semsei in Budapest. He was born on 30 Mar 1886 in the Pusztaszetlorinc neighbourhood of Budapest. Together they had two sons, Gabriel Stefan Semsei born 25 Jan 1919 in Budapest, and Tamas (Thomas) Otto Semsei born 16 Dec 1922 in Budapest. They lived at 54 Columbus utca in Budapest. We do not know what Armin did. We do not know when and how he died. We do know that Armin and Georgina (sometimes also called Stefania) were members of the Brit HaZohar B'Hungaria, i.e, Zionist revisionist group in Hungary. As was their son Tamas. On later forms to be found in the Arolsen Archives Georgina wrote that from "March 1944 to February 1945 I was hidden in a dark cellar room in Budapest", see here and here. Georgina survived the war, as did her sons. She remained in Budapest until 5 Jan 1950 when she emigrated from Hungary to Israel. She came on the boat "Comijut" (perhaps Kommeniut). In Israel she initially lived in Gedera, probably with one of her sisters. On 8 Aug 1952 she married Dr. Otto Kraus in Tel-Aviv. Dr. Otto Kraus was a chest physician. He was born in Austria on 8 Mar 1893. He was interred in Theresienstadt, survived, and immediately after the war became the official doctor for the American Joint Distribution Committee in Prague, see his obituary. In the meantime it seems that her son Gabriel had emigrated to Sydney, Australia. In March of 1955 Georgina, her husband Otto and her son Tamas also arrived in Sydney. Gabriel had a store, married and had a daughter. Sadly he died when his daughter was only 2 years old. Tamas, who was now officially Thomas, became a very successful Tyre distributor. In fact he received an award from the Israel-Australia Chamber of Commerce in 1976, and the "Medal of the Order of Australia" from the Australian government in 1997 "For Service to Business Through Marketing in the Tyre Industry". Thomas married Eva Agota. We know nothing about any children. Otto died on 8 Feb 1972 , Gabriel on 17 Nov 1977, Georgina on 11 Jul 1983 and Thomas on 3 August 2008. They are all buried in the Macquarie Park Cemetery in Sydney.

    Luiza (Lutzi) was born about 1902 in Budapest into a middle class educated family. She first married Alexander Fenyvesi of whom we know nothing. It is said that he was from a prominent family in Transylvania. Together they had a son Stefan (Pista) born on 1 Feb 1925 in Oradea, Romania. Alexander and Luiza divorced. It seems that Stefan was brought up by his mother and they lived in Subotica and Budapest. Stefan was a student. In about 1944 he was transported to KZ Mauthausen where he was put to work as a "machine fitter". He was murdered there on 18 Mar 1945. See this form from the Arolsen Archive, and the "Pages of Testimony" at the Yad VaShem website submitted by his mother and half-brother. Luiza married Arpad Hutter in about 1935. Arpad was born on 6 Apr 1891 in Visznek, Hungary. From what can be determined Arpad had been married at least twice previously. But this marrriage lasted. Arpad, via a daughter from a previous marriage, already had a granddaughter at the time of this marriage (both daughter and granddaughter were murdered in the Holocaust). Arpad came from a poor childhood, but studied and worked hard. He learnt the textile business and jointly owned and managed a textile company in Subotica with non-Jewish partners. Subotica is in Serbia, very close to the Hungarian border. It is about 100 km from Novi Sad and 200 km from Budapest. Until the end of WWI it was part of the Hungarian Kingdom. From the end of WWI it was in Yugoslavia. Arpad's business prospered and allowed the family to maintain an upper-middle class life in Subotica. Their son Tomislav (called Tommy) was born on 12 May 1936 in Budapest. (We assume Luiza went to her parents home to give birth.) The family had 3 domestic workers, a car and a driver, as well as a summer place near Budapest. Luiza went to take "the cure" from time to time. Tommy tells about being in first grade with all the children of his neighbourhood and that he had both Jewish and non-Jewish friends. But by the second grade he was in a school only with Jewish children. As WWII progressed Tommy and Luiza were moved into a Ghetto in Subotica, while Arpad was sent to a nearby work camp, but came home every few weeks. On 16 June 1944 Luiza and Tommy were shipped by cattle cars to the Strasshof camp near Vienna, having been joined by Arpad along the way. They were the lucky ones. Strasshof was not an extermination camp, see here. Jews were being held there for a possible exchange by the Nazis of Jews for material. Nonetheless it was not at all a picnic. There were major hygiene and food problems. Together with some 50-60 other Hungarian Jews they were then moved to a nearby village where the adults worked digging canals near the Danube. This was probably the Ulrichskirchen Camp. The children worked in the kitchen and had other duties. The family was there for almost one year. Tommy recalled how some locals left them potatoes and beet sugar. They were liberated on 9 May 1945. Arpad, Luiza and Tommy had survived the war. Stefan had not. They immigrated to Israel in 1948 or 1949. They lived in Gedera where they are now all buried. When Tommy arrived in Israel he was initially sent by himself to a kibbutz. However he was diagnosed with tuberculosis and spent a lengthy period of time at a sanatorium in Nes Ziona. Due to this fact he was also not conscripted into the armed forces. Tommy became a water expert and worked for the Makorot company for over 40 years. He continued to live in Gedera on the same farm as his parents. He married Aliza nee Bonomo (born in 1946), and together they had 4 children and, at last count, 13 grandchildren. Tommy's one hour interview on his life can be found on YouTube here. It is in Hebrew. Tommy died in January 2013.

    Rudolf (Rudi) was born on 26 Mar 1905 in Budapest. He married Anna Herzl who was born 11 Oct 1911 in Budapest. Here is a wedding photo. We believe they lived and worked in Novi Sad, but went often to Budapest. Anna and Rudi's son Peter was born on 12 Feb 1938 in Budapest. Anna's parents seem to have been named Adelbert (Bela) Herzl and Ela Handler Herzl. During the early war years Yugoslavia was at peace, until it was invaded and partitioned by the Axis powers (Germany, Italy, Hungary, and Bulgaria) in the spring of 1941. In these early war years efforts were made by various Jewish organizations to ship Jews to safety via Yugoslavia. Rudi's cousin Laci Guttmann (see Section 3) was very much involved in these efforts. But Rudi and other associated with the company "Guttmann and Frank" also helped. The fact that Rudi had a car was very useful. Peter with his mother, his Herzl grandparents and Rudi's mother Margit were in Budapest, where it was safer for the first period of the war. Sadly at some time in 1943 Anna died of a brain tumor. Moreover at some point, probably after Germany took over the Hungarian government in 1944, they went into hiding. They spent months in the basement of a worker of Bela Herzl. During this period Rudi was sent to a work camp, escaped, and joined the Yugoslav (Tito) partisans (the communist-led anti-fascist resistance to the Axis powers in occupied Yugoslavia during World War II). A family story is that Rudi twice had to prove he was not a Hungarian soldier. Once when he went to join the Yugoslav partisans, and then when he came back to Novi Sad after the war and was confronted by the Russian army. Both times he said he was a Jew and dropped his pants. He passed muster.  In the list of Yugoslav Jews who survived the war we find, at the same address Petra Zrinjskog 14 in Novi Sad, Rudi, his mother Margit, his cousin Imre (Mirko) along with Imre's wife and daughter, and his in-laws Bela and Ela Herzl, see here amd go to the letters "g" and "h". Rudi remarried, probably in 1946. His new wife was Katalin Bierman Lempel. She was widowed, and the mother of Tommy Lempel who became, in Israel, Tommy Lapid, a very well-known journalist and politician. It is said that Bela and Ela Herzl opposed the marriage, but to no avail. They all lived together in Novi Sad where Rudi had set up a business. He owned a small shipyard. In late 1948 Jews were permitted for a short period of time to leave Yugoslavia. Rudi, Katalin, Margit, Peter and Tommy all left. They arrived in Haifa on 26 Dec 1948, on the boat Cephalus. Rudi retrieved money he had sent to England. In Tel-Aviv he established a mushroom growing business that flourished until it did not. Rudi died on 10 Dec 1954 from throat cancer. Katalin remarried. She died on 7 July 1973. . On arrival in Israel, Peter was almost immediately sent to live on Kibbutz Kfar Szold. He was all of ten years old, a child who had already been through the Holocaust, lost his mother, and in a new country with a language he did not speak. It is said that his father and step-mother only came to visit him once every two or three months. At some point Peter moved to another kibbutz that he eventually left. He worked in the hotel industry, and then later for Bank Hapoalim. He married Ella Wertheim (she was born in 1946) and they had 3 children. Peter died on 29 Jul 2024 of cancer. He is buried in Tel-Aviv. Here is a translation of the Eulogy given by Tommy Lapid's son Yair Lapid at Peter's funeral. And here is a photo of Peter and his older stepbrother Tommy Lapid. Peter is on our right. What we have reported on here is mainly based on what we have been told by Peter's children, and on what appeared in the book "Memories After My Death: The Story of My Father, Joseph 'Tommy' Lapid", a biography of Tommy Lapid written in autobiographical form by his son Yair Lapid.

    Michelle Dyson and Allan Pinkus
