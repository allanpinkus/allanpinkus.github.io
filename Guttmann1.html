Guttmann Family


    1. Introduction:

    This is a story about a portion of the Guttmann family. We start with Isaac Guttmann and Katherine Leinkauf Guttmann. But then we mainly follow the line of Katherine's biological sons Hermann Eisner Guttmann and Alfred Eisner Guttmann. We relate what we have learnt about the firm "Guttmann and Frank". And then add some comments and references with regards to the other parts of the family. Many details of the story remain obscure. As with most family histories reconstructed from a few surviving documents, the record is incomplete and often raises as many questions as it answers. But we were struck by how much we did uncover and discover. Please feel free to contact us if you can add to or correct anything herein.


    2. Isaac and Katherine:

    Isaac Guttmann was born in Oct 1840 in the town of Reca which is near Senec. Senec is about 30 km east of Pressburg. Pressburg was the German name for the city of Bratislava, Slovakia. This entire area was, until WWI, in the Hungarian part of the Austro-Hungarian Empire. The Jews of this area spoke German, and perhaps also Hungarian. Isaac was the son of Moses Guttmann (abt 1790 – 1874 Rete, Slovakia) and Miriam Regner Guttmann (1810 – Feb 10, 1900 Rete, Slovakia). Isaac first married Regina Blau (1843 – 1887) on 8 Aug 1862 in Dunaszerdahely (about 50 km southeast of Pressburg), see their marriage registration. Regina and Isaac had two daughters. Julie, who was born on 25 Aug 1866 and Hermine (Hani), who was born on 26 Nov 1869. Both were probably born in Dunaszerdahely. Both Isaac and Regina's families seem to have been traditional Orthodox Jews. The story that was told in the family was that Regina died young, leading Isaac to remarry. While it is true that Regina died while fairly young, that is aged 44, (see her death record) the fact is that Isaac and Regina divorced. This is noted both on Isaac’s second marriage record (see here) and on Regina’s death record. It seems that this was a family secret. We do not know the reason for the divorce. But note that the daughters were in the care of Isaac. So perhaps she had a mental breakdown or terrible postnatal depression. We can only speculate.

    Katherine (Katalin) Leinkauf was born in 1836 in Pressburg. Katherine was, from we can determine, one of six children of Hermann Leinkauf and Bella Weiss (abt 1796, Floss, Germany – 9 Dec 1852, Pressburg). Four of Katherine's siblings emigrated from Pressburg to Mobile, Alabama, over some years in the mid 19th century. They had an uncle Abraham Weiss who had previously emigrated from Floss to Mobile. Katherine and a sister named Minna remained in Pressburg. On 12 June 1864 Katherine married Max Eisner in Ganserndof, which is also near Pressburg, (see their marriage record). Max Eisner was born about 1828 in Pressburg. We have no details as to when he died, but it was before 1874. Katherine and Max had two sons, namely Hermann, born 21 Mar 1865 in Vienna, and Alfred, born 19 Feb 1866, also in Vienna.

    In May of 1874, in Pressburg, the widow Katherine Leinkauf Eisner married the divorced Isaac Guttmann, (see their marriage record). The marriage was conducted at the Neolog (or conservative) synagogue in Pressburg. Marrying in a Neolog synagogue seems a significant break from Orthodox tradition. But this was becoming more and more common as Neolog Judaism appealed primarily to middle-class, urbanized, and culturally integrated Hungarian Jews. Isaac officially adopted Katherine's two sons, and thus their family name became Guttmann. Katherine and Isaac then had two additional daughters, namely Berta born on 7 Apr 1875 in Pressburg, and Malvina born on 7 Oct 1876 also in Pressburg. Katherine and Isaac raised all 6 children, i.e., Katherine's two boys with Max Eisner, Isaac's two daughters with Regina Blau, and their two joint daughters.

    Isaac and Katherine moved to Novi Sad, in the province of Vojvodina in what is today Serbia where Isaac acquired large land holdings. They owned a large working quarry at Krcedin, about 40km from Novi Sad, where the land included fields, meadows, vineyards and buildings. They also owned a salt mine just outside of Budapest. More about all this in Section ??. By the time Isaac died in 1906, Isaac and Katherine were living in Váci Street, a prime location in the middle of Budapest, close to the Danube river. Isaac headed the firm I. Guttmann and Sons. What exactly this was is unclear.

    Isaac died in Budapest on 4 Jan 1906 at the age of 65, (see here). An announcement of his death appeared in Pester Lloyd a German-language daily newspaper published in Budapest, (see here and here). Katherine died of a stroke on 22 Mar 1915 in Budapest at the age of 79, (see here). Katherine and Isaac are buried in the Kozma Street Jewish cemetery in Budapest. In 2025 the grave was restored by descendants, namely by Michelle Dyson and her family. From their address, the death announcements, etc...., we see that this was an upper middle class Jewish bourgeois family.


    2. Hermann and his offspring:

    As stated above, Hermann was born Hermann Eisner on 21 Mar 1865 in Vienna. He and his brother Alfred were formally adopted by Isaac Guttmann and took the family name of Guttmann. Here and here are the adoption papers. While we have been unable to decipher a date on these papers, we assume it was about 1874. In 1895 Hermann married Margit Ungar. Here is their wedding certificate. We were only able to decipher the year. Margit was born on 17 Oct 1876 in Gyékényes, Hungary. She was the daughter of Samuel Ungar and Paula Kremsier Ungar. Hermann's main business seems to have centered in Novi Sad and environs. We will relate more on that later. But he and Margit were often to be found in Budapest. In fact these two cities were only about 300 km from each other. Hermann and Margit had 4 children, all of whom were born in Budapest. They were Georgina (Gyorgyi) born 20 Aug 1899, Luiza (Lutzi) born about 1902, Rudolf (Rudi) born 26 Mar 1905 and Veronia (Vera) born 4 Dec 1910. We have here a copy of Hermann's Yugoslavian citizenship certificate from 1934. His height is listed as 1.68 m, his face is oval in shape, and his hair is grey. In 1939 Hermann and Margit lived at Ulica Petra Zrinskog 2 in the center of Novi Sad (today called Ulica Petra Drapsina). Hermann died on 4 Nov 1939 at the age of 74. The cause of his death is listed as arteriosclerosis cardiac paralysis. Hermann is buried in the Novi Sad Jewish Cemetery, (see here). Margit survived the war as, perhaps surprisingly, did all four of her children. Margit was for a while in Budapest, in hiding, with her grandson Peter (Rudi's son), Peter's mother Anna, and Anna's parents Bela and Ela Herzl. After the war Margit returned to Novi Sad and was with Rudi and his family. We do not know when Margit came to Israel. She is not on the passenger list of the boat that brought Rudi. Margit came to Israel with her daughter Luiza and her family on 30 June 1949 on the ship "Radnik", see here. She then lived with her daughter Luiza in Gedera, Israel. She died there in 1964 at the age of 88.

    Georgina (Gyorgi) was born on 20 Aug 1899 in Budapest. She was the eldest daughter of Hermann and Margit. On 30 Apr 1918 she married Dr. Armin (Hermann) Semsei in Budapest. (In Hungary this does not mean that he was a physician.) Armin was born on 30 Mar 1886 in the Pusztaszetlorinc neighbourhood of Budapest. Together they had two sons, Gabriel Stefan Semsei born 25 Jan 1919 in Budapest, and Tamas (Thomas) Otto Semsei born 16 Dec 1922 in Budapest. They lived at 54 Columbus utca in Budapest. We do not know what Armin did. We do not know when and how he died. We do know that Armin and Georgina (sometimes also called Stefania) were members of the Brit HaZohar B'Hungaria, i.e, the Zionist revisionist group in Hungary. As was their son Tamas. On later forms to be found in the Arolsen Archives Georgina wrote that from "March 1944 to February 1945 I was hidden in a dark cellar room in Budapest", see here and here. Georgina survived the war, as did her sons. She remained in Budapest until 5 Jan 1950 when she emigrated from Hungary to Israel. She came on the ship she lists as the "Comijut" (perhaps it was the "Kommeniut"). In Israel she initially lived in Gedera, probably with her mother and/or one of her sisters. On 8 Aug 1952 she married Dr. Otto Kraus in Tel-Aviv. Dr. Otto Kraus was a chest physician. He was born in Austria on 8 Mar 1893. He was interred in Theresienstadt, survived, and immediately after the war became the official doctor for the American Joint Distribution Committee in Prague, see his obituary. In the meantime it seems that her son Gabriel had emigrated to Sydney, Australia. In March of 1955 Georgina, her husband Otto and her son Tamas also arrived in Sydney. Gabriel had a store, married and had a daughter. Sadly he died when his daughter was only 2 years old. Tamas, who was now officially Thomas, became a very successful Tyre distributor. In fact he received an award from the Israel-Australia Chamber of Commerce in 1976, and the "Medal of the Order of Australia" from the Australian government in 1997 "For Service to Business Through Marketing in the Tyre Industry". Thomas married Eva Agota. We know nothing about any children. Otto died on 8 Feb 1972 , Gabriel on 17 Nov 1977, Georgina on 11 Jul 1983 and Thomas on 3 August 2008. They are all buried in the Macquarie Park Cemetery in Sydney.

    Luiza (Lutzi) was born about 1902 in Budapest. She first married Alexander Fenyvesi of whom we know nothing. It is said that he was from a prominent family in Transylvania. Together they had a son Stefan (Pista) born on 1 Feb 1925 in Oradea, Romania. Alexander and Luiza subsequently divorced. From what we can determine Stefan was brought up by his mother and they lived in both Subotica and Budapest. Stefan was a student. In about 1944 he was transported to KZ Mauthausen where he was put to work as a "machine fitter". He was murdered there on 18 Mar 1945. See this form from the Arolsen Archive, and the "Pages of Testimony" at the Yad VaShem website submitted by his mother and half-brother. Luiza married Arpad Hutter in about 1935. Arpad was born on 6 Apr 1891 in Visznek, Hungary. From what can be determined Arpad had been married at least twice previously. But this marrriage lasted. Arpad, via a daughter from a previous marriage, already had a granddaughter at the time of this marriage (both daughter and granddaughter were murdered in the Holocaust). Arpad came from a poor childhood, but studied and worked hard. He learnt the textile business and jointly owned and managed a textile company in Subotica with non-Jewish partners. Subotica is in Serbia, very close to the Hungarian border. It is about 100 km from Novi Sad and 200 km from Budapest. Until the end of WWI it was part of the Hungarian Kingdom. From the end of WWI it was in Yugoslavia. Their son Tomislav (called Tommy) was born on 12 May 1936 in Budapest. (We assume Luiza went to her parents home to give birth.) Arpad's business prospered and allowed the family to maintain a proper upper-middle class life in Subotica. The family had 3 domestic workers, a car and a driver, as well as a summer place near Budapest. Luiza went to take "the cure" from time to time. Tommy tells about being in first grade with all the children of his neighbourhood and that he had both Jewish and non-Jewish friends. But by the second grade he was in a school only with Jewish children. As WWII progressed Tommy and Luiza were moved into a Ghetto in Subotica, while Arpad was sent to a nearby work camp, but came home every few weeks. On 16 June 1944 Luiza and Tommy were shipped by cattle cars to the Strasshof camp near Vienna, having been joined by Arpad along the way. They were the lucky ones. Strasshof was not an extermination camp, see here. Jews were being held there for a possible exchange by the Nazis of Jews for material. Nonetheless it was a prison camp and there were major hygiene and food problems. Together with some 50-60 other Hungarian Jews they were then moved to a nearby village where the adults worked digging canals near the Danube. This was probably the Ulrichskirchen Camp. The children worked in the kitchen and had other duties. The family was there for almost a full year. Tommy recalled how some locals left them potatoes and beet sugar. They were liberated on 9 May 1945. Arpad, Luiza and Tommy had survived the war. Stefan had not. They immigrated to Israel arriving on 30 June 1949 on the ship "Radnik", see here. They lived in Gedera where they are now all buried. When Tommy arrived in Israel he was initially sent by himself to a kibbutz. However he was diagnosed with tuberculosis and spent a lengthy period of time at a sanatorium in Nes Ziona. Due to this fact he was also not conscripted into the armed forces. Tommy became a water expert and worked for the Makorot company for over 40 years. He continued to live in Gedera on the same farm as his parents. He married Aliza nee Bonomo (born in 1946), and together they had 4 children and, at last count, 13 grandchildren. Tommy's one hour interview on his life can be found on YouTube here. It is in Hebrew. Tommy died in January 2013.

    Rudolf (Rudi) was born on 26 Mar 1905 in Budapest. He married Anna Herzl who was born 11 Oct 1911 in Budapest. Here is a wedding photo. We believe they lived and worked in Novi Sad, but often went to Budapest. Anna and Rudi's son Peter was born on 12 Feb 1938 in Budapest. Anna's parents were Bela (Adelbert) Herzl (1881 Ada - 1959 Budapest) and Ela Handler Herzl (1881 Mosorin - 1965 Budapest). During the early war years Yugoslavia was at peace, until it was invaded and partitioned by the Axis powers (Germany, Italy, Hungary, and Bulgaria) in the spring of 1941. In these early war years efforts were made by various Jewish organizations to ship Jews to safety via Yugoslavia. Rudi's cousin Laci Guttmann (see Section 3) was very much involved in these efforts. But Rudi and others associated with the company "Guttmann and Frank" also helped. The fact that Rudi had a car was very useful for transporting materials and food. Peter with his mother, his Herzl grandparents and Rudi's mother Margit were in Budapest, where it was safer for the first period of the war. Sadly at some time in 1943 Anna died of a brain tumor. Moreover at some point, probably after Germany took over the Hungarian government in 1944, they went into hiding. They spent months in the basement of a worker of Bela Herzl. During this period Rudi was sent to a work camp, escaped, and joined the Yugoslav (Tito) partisans (the communist-led anti-fascist resistance to the Axis powers in occupied Yugoslavia during World War II). A family story is that Rudi twice had to prove he was not a Hungarian soldier. Once when he went to join the Yugoslav partisans, and then when he came back to Novi Sad after the war and was confronted by the Russian army. Both times he said he was a Jew and dropped his pants. He passed muster.  In the list of Yugoslav Jews who survived the war we find, at the same address Petra Zrinjskog 14 in Novi Sad, Rudi, his mother Margit, his cousin Imre (Mirko) along with Imre's wife and daughter, and his in-laws Bela and Ela Herzl (see here). Rudi remarried, probably in 1946. His new wife was Katalin Bierman Lempel. She was widowed, and the mother of Tommy Lempel who became, in Israel, Tommy Lapid, a very well-known journalist and politician. It is said that Bela and Ela Herzl opposed the marriage, but to no avail. They all lived together in Novi Sad where Rudi had set up a business. He owned a small shipyard. In late 1948 Jews were permitted, for a short period of time, to leave Yugoslavia. Rudi, Katalin, Margit, Peter and Tommy all left. They arrived in Haifa on 26 Dec 1948, on the boat "Dromit", see here, formerly named "Kefalos". See on that list also his son Peter, wife Katalin, her son Tomislav, but not his mother. (Also his cousin Imre and his daughter Suzana.) Rudi retrieved money he had sent to England. In Tel-Aviv he established a mushroom growing business that flourished until it did not. Rudi died on 10 Dec 1954 from throat cancer. Katalin remarried. She died on 7 July 1973. On arrival in Israel, Peter was almost immediately sent to live on Kibbutz Kfar Szold. He was all of ten years old, a child who had already been through the Holocaust, lost his mother, and in a new country with a language he did not speak. It is said that his father and step-mother only came to visit him once every two or three months. At some point Peter moved to another kibbutz that he eventually left. He worked in the hotel industry, and then later for Bank Hapoalim. He married Ella Wertheim (she was born in 1946) and they had 3 children. Peter died on 29 Jul 2024 of cancer. He is buried in Tel-Aviv. Here is a translation of the Eulogy given by Tommy Lapid's son Yair Lapid at Peter's funeral. And here is a photo of Peter and his older stepbrother Tommy Lapid. Peter is on our right. What we have reported on here is mainly based on what we have been told by Peter's children, and on what appeared in the book "Memories After My Death: The Story of My Father, Joseph 'Tommy' Lapid", a biography of Tommy Lapid written in autobiographical form by his son Yair Lapid.

    Veronica (Vera) was born on 4 Dec 1910 in Budapest. She married Alfred (Fredi) Weiss on 5 Oct 1930 in Budapest. Fredi was born on 10 Apr 1900 in Bánovce nad Bu, Slovakia. They had two daughters. Hella, who was born on 10 Aug 1932 in Vienna. Here is her birth certificate. And Shoshana (Susi), who was born on 15 Nov 1936 in Budapest. Fredi ran (but did not own) a farm in the small village of Kamanova near Topolcany, Slovakia. For much of the war they were unmolested. In fact they were issued a "permission document" which said that what Fredi and Vera were doing, namely farming, was essential to the Slovak economy, and so the family could not and should not be shipped out. This kept them safe, until it did not. They were the only Jews in the village. And they seem to have gotten on well with their neighbours. This situation continued until September of 1944. But then they had to go into hiding. In fact they hid in their own house, in a cellar, during the day. According to Hella they played a lot of cards and were able to get out at night. However at some point someone informed on them to the Germans. The family was sent to the Sered camp where Fredi was beaten, and Hella was almost raped. It was already 1945. Finally on 12 Mar 1945 they were transported to Theresienstadt. And at some later date they were then put on a transport to an extermination camp. But the train was bombed and some people in the forward carriages were killed. Fortunately they were near the end of the train. The commander had no idea as to what to do, and so brought them back to Theresienstadt. In May 1945 Theresienstadt was liberated by the Russians. The family returned to Kamanova. The house had been ransacked. But their domestic help had come every so often to collect their things, which they then got back. The family stayed on in Slovakia for a few years. They even "Slovakizied" their family name from Weiss to Verny in 1946, see here. In 1955 in Israel they officially changed it back to Weiss. Hella Weiss came to Israel in Feb 1949 with Aliyat HaNoar and HaShomer Hatzair. She enlisted in the army and became a nurse. Vera and Fredi arrived in Israel with Shoshana (Susi) in Nov 1949. They went to Gedera because Fredi had relatives there. They initially rented a farm. But they then bought it together with Margit, Lutzi, Arpad and Tommy, partially with money that the family had managed to deposit in Switzerland before the war. In addition, in the 1950's Vera and Lutzi ran a laundry. Eventually they also received some German reparations. Fredi died on 15 Nov 1980 and is buried in Gedera, while Vera died on 8 Apr 1984 and is also buried in Gedera. Hella met and married Marcel (Mordechai) Kornblum on 21 Mar 1954, see here. Marcel was born 12 Jun 1923 in Krakow. He had had a difficult war. Was in the camps and contracted tuberculosis that affected him his entire life. In Israel he eventually became a police officer. Together Hella and Mordechai had two sons and a daughter. For many years they lived in Petach Tikva. But in 1973 they transferred to Jerusalem. Mordechai died on 14 Feb 2005 and Hella died on 26 Feb 2018. Both are buried in Jerusalem. Shoshana married Reuven Drori (Mauskopf) who was born on 19 Aug 1936 in Nitra, Slovakia. They were members of Kibbutz Dan. On shlichut in New York in the early 1980s it was discovered that Shoshana had cancer. They returned to Kibbutz Dan where Shoshana died on 8 Feb 1983. Together they had a son, who died as a baby, and three girls. None of Fredi and Vera's descendants lived in Gedera. So when Vera died the Hutter family bought the Weiss family's part of the farm. (The farm is all of one and a half duman, which is about a third of an acre.) Hella memories were recorded, in Hebrew, here.

    3. Alfred and his offspring:

    Alfred was born on 19 Feb 1866 in Vienna, and with his brother Hermann, was formally adopted by Isaac Guttmann and so took the family name Guttmann. Alfred married Eugenia (Jenny) Stein on 25 Mar 1900 in Baja, Hungary. Here is their marriage certificate. Jenny was born on 13 Feb 1881 in Katymar (not too far from Baja). Together they had three sons, all born in Budapest. They were Imre (Mirko) born 2 Mar 1901, Pal (Palve) born 29 Sep 1902, and Laszlo (Ladislav, Laci) born many years later on 5 Jan 1914. Alfred died on 18 Oct 1925 after a long illness. Here is the announcement of his death as it appeared in Pester Lloyd. This announcement says that he is to be buried in Budapest, but he is also listed on the same gravestone as his wife who is buried in Novi Sad. (Also listed on that gravestone is his son Palve (Pal) who was murdered in the Holocaust in the Ukraine.) His wife Jenny died on 4 Aug 1935 in Novi Sad. Here is data from JewishGen. In addition, we have a letter from the Jewish community in Novi Sad that states that just prior to the war the house at Vojvođanska 5, Novi Sad, was jointly owned by the three sons. So this was most probably where Alfred and Jenny lived.

    Pal (Palve) was born 29 Sep 1902 in Budapest. Here is his birth registration. He married Lydia Loschitz. She was born in Novi Sad on 26 May 1909. Their daughter Katarina Eva Guttman was born in Novi Sad on 21 Feb 1933. Theirs is a tragic story. Pal was conscripted into the labor battalion of the Hungarian army. Most of those conscripted perished or were murdered. Pal was shot and killed by a Hungarian Nazi collaborator on 28 Feb 1943 in Khrenovoye, Ukraine. His wife and daughter lived in Budapest. They were taken to the Danube and shot by Hungarian Nazi collaborators on 15 Jan 1945. The source for this information are the three "Pages of Testimony" submitted by Pal's younger brother Laszlo (Ladislav, Laci) in 1998. See here, here, and here.
