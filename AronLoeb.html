The Family of Aron Loeb Boscowitz


    1. Introduction:

    Aron Loeb Boscowitz was born in about 1787 in Floss, Oberpfalz, Bavaria to Abraham Boskowitz and Mariana nee Schwarz. He was a grandson of Aron Boskowitz (1720-1784) the "founder" of the Boskowitz line in Floss, after whom he was named. It is assumed that Aron Boskowitz, who came to Floss in about 1740, was from Boskovice in Moravia (in what is now the Czech Republic). (The German name for Boskovice was Boskowitz.) Aron Loeb Boscowitz was a first cousin of my great-great-grandfather Abraham Anschel Boscowitz.

    Aron Loeb Boscowitz became a successful merchant whose business interests were centered in St. Thomas in the Danish West Indies. There he formed a relationship with Marthonne Garnier, a French Catholic refugee from Haiti, and together they had a son, George Pierre Boscowitz. Aron Loeb died on Sept. 24, 1838 at the age of 51 in Hamburg, Germany. He is buried in the Jewish Portuguese Cemetery in Altona (very near Hamburg). His estate took years to be probated. There were some legacies but his heirs were his son and his siblings. The son received about half the estate, while the other half was essentially divided among his siblings. Although precise modern equivalents are difficult to calculate, the estate appears to have represented wealth amounting to several million dollars in present-day terms.

    Aron Loeb had six siblings who survived to adulthood: five brothers and one sister. The brothers were Lazarus (1773-1857), Elias (1775-1859), Jakob Abraham (c. 1777 - 1850), Moses (1790-1867) and Samuel (??). The sister was Breindel (1782-1869) (who married David Nathan Grunbaum (1777-1860)).

    George Pierre was only eleven years old when his father died in 1838. A decade later, having reached adulthood, he left St. Thomas with his mother and settled in Paris, where he spent the remainder of his life. He was a rentier (a man of independent means) and became an homme des lettres (an educated, scholarly person who makes his living, if needed, through literary and intellectual pursuits). He was known as Arnold Boscowitz. He wrote two books and many articles for various publications. In 1886 he was awarded a prize by the Académie Française for one of his books. He had 4 or 5 children, and has descendants who are alive today.

    In what follows we detail what we know about Aron Loeb, his life and death, and his descendants. Many aspects of the story remain obscure. As with most family histories reconstructed from surviving documents, the record is incomplete and often raises as many questions as it answers.


    2. Background:

    A bit of background regarding the Danish West Indies, Altona and Floss.

    The Danish West Indies, also known as the Danish Virgin Islands, was established in 1672 as a mercantile adventure, and became a formal Danish colony only in 1754. It consisted of four relatively small islands.

    The economy of the Danish West Indies effectively depended on slavery. Danish colonisers in the West Indies aimed to exploit the profitable triangular trade, involving the export of firearms and other manufactured goods to Africa in exchange for slaves, who were then transported to the Caribbean to work the sugar plantations. Caribbean colonies, in turn, exported sugar, rum and molasses to Denmark. After a rebellion, slavery was officially abolished in 1848, leading to the near economic collapse of the plantations.

    In 1917 the 3 larger islands were purchased by the United States for 25 million dollars, and became known as the United States Virgin Islands. The 4th island, Water Island, was bought by the US Government in 1944, and became part of the US Virgin Islands in 1996.

    The Danish West Indies were inhabited by many different cultures, and each had its own traditions and religions. Freedom of religion was partially granted to the colonies to help settle the islands, as there was a shortage of willing settlers from Europe. This resulted in a large proportion being Dutch and British immigrants fleeing religious persecution.

    Jews, along with Catholics, were granted freedom of religion in 1685. In 1796 the Jews of the main town of Charlotte Amalie on the island of Saint Thomas founded a synagogue. Only nine Jewish families belonged to the congregation in 1801. But by 1803 it had increased to 22, with arrivals from England, France, and other Caribbean islands. At its peak, around 1850, the Jewish population numbered 400. Unlike other colonies St. Thomas featured a global mix of English, French, German, Italian, Spanish, American, Jewish, and Danish residents running major import houses. One of the earliest colonial governors, Gabriel Milan, was a Sephardic Jew.

    Aron Loeb is buried in Altona, and we assume that this is not by chance. Altona is now a suburb of Hamburg in northern Germany. But that was not always the case. Altona, unlike Hamburg, was in the province of Holstein. From 1640 until 1864 it was under the control of the King of Denmark (although not part of Denmark proper). Jewish merchants in Altona played a crucial role in the economic and cultural development of the region, operating as wholesalers, bankers, and importers of colonial goods (sugar, coffee, tobacco). Altona, under Danish rule, offered a more tolerant and favorable environment than neighboring Hamburg.

    Aron Loeb Boscowitz died on Sept. 24, 1838, and it is recorded that he was 51 years of age at the time of his death. So we assume he was born circa 1787. He was born in Floss, Oberpfalz in the Electorate of Bavaria. Floss is a small market town about 10 km northeast of Weiden. (Its official name is Markt Floss.) Floss was one of a very, very few (two or three) towns in the Oberpfalz where Jews were permitted to reside if they had permission (were granted a Schutz). To be more precise Aron Loeb was born on the Judenberg [Jew's Hill] that was "next" to Floss. Jews were not permitted to live in Floss. The Judenberg was separately administered and was only incorporated into Floss in 1870. There were also numerous restrictions and taxes imposed upon the Jews. There were restrictions on residency, occupation, marriage, etc. The number of Jews that lived in Floss was never very large. At the beginning of the 19th century it is estimated that there were about 50 Jewish families, i.e., about 200 Jews in Floss. Most were in "business" (merchants). But the local authorities were insistent that Jewish youth "learn proper trades" (e.g., combmaker, butcher, furrier, soapmaker etc.). But it seemed, according to the authorities, that Jewish youths learned professions, but only in appearance. They used them as a pretext for trade, which they cannot be made to give up. For more about the Jewish community of Floss, see here and the links therein.


    3. Anthoni & Boscowitz:

    We are not certain as to when Aron Loeb first came to St. Thomas in the Danish West Indies. But from sometime in 1820 to May 1823 he was in a partnership there, as a merchant, in the enterprise Manuel & Boscowitz. This was dissolved in May 1823, see here.

    Aron Loeb then entered into a partnership with George Daniel (G. D.) Anthoni. This partnership was the basis of the firm of Anthoni & Boscowitz. It lasted from May 1823 until his death. We know of partnership contracts dated May 16, 1823, July 9, 1827 and March 31, 1835. The commercial contract of March 31, 1835 was then formally extended in June 1838 to last through the end of 1839. (It is very probable that at that time Aron Loeb was sick and was aware of the fact that he did not have long to live.) It is written that the trading firm Anthoni & Boscowitz, conducted business partly on the island of St. Thomas, partly in Hamburg, and partly in foreign places, according to the agreement concluded between Anthoni and Boscowitz. In fact, it was mainly a St. Thomas trading firm.

    With regard to Anthoni & Boscowitz we have an inventory, a list of accounts receivable (debtors), and accounts payable (creditors). There are also advertisements that appeared in local newspapers. The inventory of the warehouses of Messrs. Anthoni & Boscowitz was conducted on Dec. 31, 1839. Recall that Aron Loeb died in Sept. 1838, over a year previously. Anthoni & Boscowitz was then in the process of liquidation. As such we assume that this was not the standard inventory.

    The inventory, see here, tells us that Anthoni & Boscowitz were merchant wholesale importers of food, drink and sundry items. There are 129 items listed in this inventory. These include many different types of wines, liqueurs and cheeses, butter, preserves, tea, oil, rice, pasta, spices, biscuits, flour, raisins, barrels of pork, vinegar, salt, sardines, tobacco, as well as bottles, chairs, mattresses, candles, soap, paper and the like. In addition, there is one slave named Jean. Each of the 129 items are priced. The total value of the inventory is given as $25,968. (The slave Jean was valued as $150.)

    A word about the currency in use and its worth. There were various currencies used at that time in the Danish West Indies. The formal currency was called the "Rigsdaler". The Danish West Indies Rigsdaler was equal to 80% of a Danish Rigsdaler. More importantly, in 1838 the Spanish dollar (piece of eight) was more commonly used and was used interchangeably with the Danish West Indian Rigsdaler though they were technically different accounting units. Different sources give different estimates on how much buying power a unit of the above currencies had in, say, US dollars today. But a figure of 37 to 1 is often given. At this rate the inventory had a value of about $1,000,000 in today's money. (It is written that at that time a laborer had a yearly wage of between 80 to 100 Rigsdaler, while a merchant earned in the neighborhood of 500 to 1,000 Rigsdaler a year. So this ratio of 37 to 1 does not tell the full story.)

    The list of debtors (accounts receivable),see here, at the close of 1839 includes about 170 items. Many were ship captains. But there were also people (and firms) from a very broad Caribbean and northern South American commercial network. The place names listed include the towns Mayaguez, Humacao, Patillas, Arecibo, Naguabo, Cayey, Guayama, Manati and Ponce, all in Puerto Rico; Cumana, Ocamo, Barcelona, Puerto Cabello and Rio Caribe in Venezuela; Rio Hacha, Agostura and Carthagena in Colombia; as well Guadeloupe, Trinidad, St. Lucia, St. Croix, and other Caribbean islands. There were also over 30 persons and firms located in St. Thomas. The total value of the accounts receivable was close to $100,000 (local currency), so about 3.7 million US dollars in today's money.

    Of course, as is always the case, not all these debts could and would be collected. And the firm also had debts of its own. In September of 1841 a list was put together of bad debts (uncollectible assets). That is, it was considered doubtful if much money would ever be collected from them. This amounted to almost $30,000 (local currency). As to the debts of Anthoni & Boscowitz, discounting what was "owed" to both Anthoni and Boscowitz and the profits, the amount came to about $11,000 (local currency), see here. The money was mainly owed to people or firms in St. Thomas, but there were also two firms from Boston as well as three from France. Thus, overall, the net value in the firm of Anthoni & Boscowitz was on the order of $60,000 (local currency) to be divided between Anthoni and Boscowitz.

    We have two other tidbits of information with regards to Aron Loeb. The first is that he joined the Freemason Lodge of St. Thomas in March of 1830, see here. This was to be expected of a merchant of means who was interested in integration and acceptance by the surrounding society. The other thing we have is a "Subscription List for the Building of a Synagogue" by the Hebrew congregation of St. Thomas. This list appeared in April of 1833 in the local newspaper, the "St. Thomae Tidende", one of the earliest and most prominent newspapers in the Caribbean. On this subscription list we see that A. Boscowitz contributed $50 (a significant amount), and his partner G. D. Anthoni contributed $16, see here. It is doubtful that Anthoni was Jewish.


    4. The Will:

    Aron Loeb Boscowitz's last will was written on May 8, 1838 in St. Thomas, at the office of the public notary (Notarius Publicus) Conrad C. Moe.

    The estate was handled by the Danish courts both in St. Thomas and Copenhagen. Because of the size and international character of the estate (some of the heirs lived outside Denmark and its territories), it took many years (about 10 years) to be fully probated. Final estate payments seem to have been recorded in 1847, although most was distributed long before that. There was a significant amount of correspondence with the Royal Council, a need for government approvals, public notices in newspapers (this was a legal requirement), and taxes to be paid.

    In 1840 there appeared official notices in various newspapers calling on all creditors to submit their claims within a legal deadline, or else lose their right to payment.

    What follows is a translation the royal proclamation that was published in various newspaper concerning the estate of Aron Loeb Boscowitz and the dissolved firm Anthoni & Boscowitz. As stated therein this same proclamation appeared in newspapers in Copenhagen, Altona and the Danish West Indies. Here is the proclamation, in German, that appeared in the Altonaer Mercur, and here is this same proclamation, in Danish, that appeared in Dansk Vestindisk Regierings Avis (the Danish West Indian Government Gazette).

    By virtue of a most gracious Royal authorization, thus reads:

    We, CHRISTIAN the Eighth, by the Grace of God King of Denmark, the Wends and the Goths, Duke of Schleswig, Holstein, Stormarn, Dithmarschen, Lauenburg and Oldenburg, make known: Since, upon the most humble petition and request of our faithful Privy Councillor Adolph Stenersen, on behalf of the executors of the estate of the deceased merchant Aron Loeb Boscowitz, merchant George D. Anthoni, the city captain H. W. Precht, and deputy fire director and merchant Seligmann Rothschild, all of our island of St. Thomas in America, We have most graciously approved and permitted, and hereby do approve and permit, that the said executors, by means of public proclamation, under penalty of exclusion and perpetual silence (sub poena praeclusi et perpetui silentii), summon all creditors of the deceased merchant Aron Loeb Boscowitz, as well as the creditors of the dissolved trading firm Anthoni & Boscowitz, in which the deceased had been a partner, whether residing in Our dominions and lands or abroad, with a notice period of one year and one day from the date hereof, and that this proclamation be inserted three times in the Copenhagen newspapers and three times in the Altona newspapers, namely in the Kopenhagener Politische Zeitung and the Kopenhagener Adressecomptoirs-Nachrichten, and in the Altonaer Mercur, and likewise, with a notice period of three months from the date hereof, be published in Our West Indian colonies and inserted three times in the Danish West Indian Government Gazette; so that all persons residing or present in the aforesaid lands who are creditors of the deceased merchant Aron Loeb Boscowitz, as well as creditors of the dissolved firm Anthoni & Boscowitz, in which the deceased had been a partner, may come forward with their claims and present and prove them before the aforesaid executors within the prescribed time; and should the said executors find it necessary, by virtue of Our gracious authorization, to refer disputed claims to Our West Indian Debt Liquidation Commissioners, they shall be entitled to do so and obtain judgment thereon; so that the proclamation shall not prejudice valid claims, and no rightful claims shall be forfeited. Therefore We have graciously resolved to issue this letter. Given under Our Royal Seal.

    L. S. By His Royal Majesty's most gracious command. The Royal Danish Government. St. Croix, 26 March 1840. J. Søbøtker David Hoskler C. B. Petersen (as acting councillors) H. P. W. Petersen

    Public Summons

    By virtue of the foregoing royal authorization, the creditors of the deceased merchant Aron Loeb Boscowitz, as well as those of the dissolved firm Anthoni & Boscowitz, in which the deceased was a partner, are hereby summoned and cited, sub poena praeclusi et perpetui silentii, that is, under penalty of exclusion and perpetual silence, to present and substantiate any claims they may have against the estate of the said merchant Aron Loeb Boscowitz or against the dissolved firm Anthoni & Boscowitz, in which the deceased was a partner, within the period prescribed in the royal authorization, before the undersigned executors of the estate, and to prove them with sufficient evidence. Likewise, with legal proof, those creditors whose claims are disputed must appear and safeguard their rights during the proceedings for distribution.

    St. Thomas, 7 April 1840. G. D. Anthoni H. W. Precht S. Rothschild

    Note that creditors were given one year and a day if residing in Denmark, Schleswig-Holstein, or elsewhere abroad, while three months months if residing in the Danish West Indies. Furthermore this publication appeared more than 18 months after Aron Loeb Boscowitz's death, showing how long the estate administration continued and how widely notice had to be circulated throughout the Danish monarchy and overseas possessions. Finally one of the executors, namely his former partner G. D. Anthoni, died in 1841 and was replaced by A. Robles. This also did not help expedite the process.

    The will is lengthy and at times difficult to follow. The bottom line is that there were legacies/bequests, and what was left in the estate was then divided between Aron Loeb's heirs, namely his son George Pierre who received almost half the estate, and his brothers Lazarus, Elias, Moses and Samuel and his sister Breindel, all living in Floss, who divided the other half.

    The estate included: Aron Loeb's share of the firm Anthoni & Boscowitz, a house in Charlotte Amalie (the main city of St. Thomas) at Crystal Gade 12, perhaps another house in St. Thomas on Kronprindsens Gade (that is at the moment unclear), as well as money invested with bankers. There is also mentioned a gold watch, jewelry, personal items, and cash gifts. The administration of the estate involved numerous government duties, notarial fees, and legal expenses. The principal distributions recorded in the probate proceedings were:

    1. Marthonne Garnier 3,150
    2. Charles Darampé 500
    3. J. L. Maduro, Jr. 400
    4. Lazarus Boscowitz 2,438
    5. Elias Boscowitz 2,438
    6. Moses Boscowitz 2,438
    7. Breindel Boscowitz 2,438
    married to David Nathan Grunbaum
    8. Samuel Boscowitz 2,438
    9. Johanna Breisach 800
    married to Samuel Breisack
    10. George Pierre Boscowitz 14,198
    Total: 31,238

    But this was the gross amount. There was an additional 4% government tax on all these bequests.

    Garnier, Darampé, Maduro and Breisach received legacies. The other persons listed here are the heirs.


    5. Marthonne Garnier:

    Marthonne Garnier was born probably between 1788 and 1791 (depending on which record one reads) in Jacmei, Haiti. She was the daughter of Jean Garnier and Louise Massé. She died on April 27, 1877, at the home of her son George Pierre Arnold Boscowitz at 9 Rue Nicolo in (16e), Paris, France, see here.

    As a consequence of the Haitian Revolution (1791-1804) many of the French settlers in Haiti fled. Some, like Marthonne, moved to St. Thomas in the Danish West Indies. Her first husband was Philippe Darampé with whom she had a son Charles Henri Darampé, born about 1817 in St Thomas.

    At some point Marthonne Garnier appears to have entered into a marriage contract or similar civil agreement with Aron Loeb Boscowitz. He was Jewish and she was Catholic. No religious marriage appears to have taken place. We assume there was a civil marriage contract (a prenuptial property agreement) entirely separate from a church or synagogue. In the probated will of Aron Loeb there is a passage about the rights and claims which may arise in connection with Marthonne Garnier, according to the marriage contract, and there was no royal authorization for the contracting of marriage, but that the union entered into was nevertheless considered valid.

    Their son George Pierre Boscowitz was born on Dec. 17, 1826 in St. Thomas.

    On May 8, 1838, Aron Loeb made a will in St. Thomas, in which is written:

    4th. It is ordered that Mrs. Marthonne Garnier shall, for the remainder belonging to the estate and still in her possession, render and submit a complete account, together with all supporting vouchers and proofs, and moreover surrender all books and papers pertaining thereto.

    5th. It is stated that Mrs. Marthonne Garnier, born in Jacmel, Haiti, had brought into her marriage with the deceased Aron Loeb Boscowitz certain property, jewels, and effects, and that these were claimed by her as her own separate property, not as assets of the estate.

    a. The articles claimed by her include jewels, gold objects, trinkets, and other personal effects, said to have belonged to her before or independently of the marriage.

    b. It is further stated that Charles Darampé, son of Marthonne Garnier, together with the slave boy Andres, and certain household effects and personal articles, are connected with this claim and are to be treated according to the testamentary provisions and the evidence submitted.

    c. The court records that these matters require proof and accounting, because the executors must determine what belongs to the estate and what belongs separately to Marthonne Garnier.

    d. Therefore, the effects claimed by Marthonne Garnier are not simply to be divided with the estate, but are to be examined, valued, and, where her claim is established, delivered or credited to her separately.

    The domestic establishment was a house at Crystal Gade 12, Charlotte Amalie, St. Thomas.

    In addition the will contains the following:

    7th It is further stated that the deceased had left behind his natural son, George Pierre Boscowitz, born the 17th December 1826 here in St. Thomas, and with the said child begotten with Mrs. Marthonne Garnier; likewise should the said child, according to the testament made by the deceased, receive all that to him in money has been assigned, and should the management thereof, until the child becomes of age, be conducted according to the directions prescribed.

    Marthonne Garnier was not an heir of Aron Loeb. She was given a legacy, although some undetermined problems later arose concerning what belonged to the estate and what belonged to her personally.

    On May 8, 1840 she signed the following statement: "I the undersigned Marthonne Garnier with Curator, do hereby acknowledge to have received from the Executors of the late Aron Loeb Boscowitz, the legacy bequeathed to me by the will of the said deceased." She received from the estate $3,000 in cash, household effects and trinkets: valued at the sum of $100, and a mulatto boy named Andres valued at $50 (whom she later sold for $150). Thus the legacy was valued at $3,150, from which was deducted the 4% Danish tax.

    At some point the probate talks about George Pierre being governed under the rules concerning "children born outside marriage.” And that Marthonne Garnier formally acknowledged receipt of funds for his upbringing and maintenance. But the probate never says how much and when she was paid.

    Marthonne Garnier, after the death of Aron Loeb, lived at Crystal Gade 12. On October 3, 1840, she purchased the house from the estate putting up $1200 and owing the estate another $1200. It is written that she bought the property at below market value. The 1841 slave census has her owning 3 slaves, a (male) cook, a houseboy and a housegirl. The 1846 slave census has her owning 4 slaves (all different from the previous ones), two female cooks, a houseboy and a housegirl. Slavery was officially abolished two years later in 1848. The 1846 census of free people has her living at Crystal Gade 12 with her son Charles Darampé, an elderly widow, a 50 year old seamstress, a 35 year old male cook, and a 9 year old male child. It is to be assumed that she rented rooms to some or all of these people. Marthonne is listed as a rentiere (a person of independent means who lives entirely off unearned income from investments and the like).

    In December of 1846 a nephew of Aron Loeb Boscowitz, namely Dr. Marcus Hirsch Boscowitz (son of Aron Loeb's brother Lazarus) came to St. Thomas. His visit was at least partially connected with the still-unsettled estate. He was given a power-of-attorney in this matter by his parents. He initially stayed at Crystal Gade 12, at the residence of Mrs. Boscowitz. He seems to have been in St. Thomas for over a year. The house was put up for rent, with a lot of fine furniture in May of 1848. No further information has yet been located.

    Little is presently known about George Pierre's childhood, education, or early upbringing. In June 1848 when George Pierre was already 21 years of age, he together with his mother Marthonne Garnier, now called Madame Marthe Boscowitz, and his half brother Charles Darampe, travelled via Jamaica on the ship Teviot to Southampton, England. George Pierre, Marthe and Charles subsequently settled in Paris.

    It should be noted with regard to George Pierre's inheritance, and it was a substantial sum, that formal legal majority according to law was 25 years of age. But practical financial emancipation may have occurred around 21.

    As stated previously Marthe Boscowitz died in her late 80s on April 27, 1877. She is buried in the Cimetière de Montmartre (18e), Paris, France.


    6. Charles Henri Darampé:

    According to his death certificate, Charles Henri Darampé was born in 1816 or 1817 in St. Thomas. His father was Philippe Darampé and his mother Marthonne Garnier. He died at the age of 33 on May 18, 1850 in Paris, France, and is buried in the Cimetière de Montmartre (18e), Paris. He is listed as unmarried and a "rentier" (having independent means).

    The 1846 census of free people in St. Thomas has him living with his mother at her house at Crystal Gade 12 in Charlotte Amalie. He is recorded there as a 28 year old unmarried catholic, a citizen who was born in St. Thomas, and a clerk. He traveled with his mother and half-brother George Pierre Boscowitz via Jamaica on the ship Teviot to Southampton, England, in June 1848. On the manifest he is listed as a merchant. (See previous links.)

    As a stepson of Aron Loeb Boscowitz he was given a bequest of $500.


    7. J. L. Maduro, Jr.:

    J. L. Maduro, Jr., was, according to the probate, a godson of Aron Loeb Boscowitz. He was left a legacy of $400. He was also requested by Aron Loeb, in his will, to be a clerk for his estate.

    In the probate we have (in English), dated August 1, 1840: I, the undersigned J. L. Maduro, Jr. do hereby acknowledged to have received from the Executors of the late Mr. Aron Loeb Boscowitz, the legacy bequeathed to me by the Will of the said deceased, dated 8th of May, amounting to $400.


    8. Lazarus, Elias, Moses, Breindel and Samuel Boscowitz:

    These are five of the six siblings of Aron Loeb Boscowitz. We know that they were all born in Floss, and that Lazarus (1773-1857), Elias (1775-1859), Moses (1790-1867) and Breindel (1782-1869) also died in Floss. Of Samuel we have no information other than that in the probate. Samuel seems to have moved to Gräfenberg and then later to Pest (part of today's Budapest).

    Lazarus and Elias married and had children. They have living descendants. We know that Breindel married David Nathan Gruenbaum, but we have no knowledge of any children. Nor do we know anything more regarding Moses and Samuel.


    9. Johanna Breisach:

    Jacob Boscowitz (c. 1777-1850) was a sibling of Aron Loeb and was alive when Aron Loeb died in 1838. But he is not listed as an heir. The reason for his exclusion as an heir is not presently known. Jacob was a merchant and had moved with his wife and only child Johanna to Pest (today part of Budapest). Johanna Boscowitz Breisach (c. 1816-1894) married Samuel Breisach (1814-1880) in 1837 in Pest. Together they had, we believe, seven children. In 1845 Jacob and his wife Susanne, together with Johanna, her husband Samuel and their children, all converted to Catholicism and also Hungarized their family names. Jacob and Susanne Boscowitz became Karl and Susanna Marie Boskanyi. Samuel and Johanna Breisach became Franz and Anna Bresanyi. In the will Johanna was left 2000 Bavarian guilders, amounting to $800.


    10. George Pierre Boscowitz and Family:

    George Pierre Boscowitz was born on Dec 17, 1826 in St. Thomas. His parents were Aron Loeb Boscowitz and Marthonne Garnier. See sections "1. Introduction", "4. The Will" and "5. Marthonne Garnier" for further details. As stated therein, George Pierre was 11 years old when his father died. We do not know anything about him, e.g. where he was educated, until at the age of 21 he, together with his mother, and half-brother Charles Darampé traveled from Jamaica to England, and then to Paris, France. His father sometimes went by the name of Arnold, and it seems that at some point he also "became" Arnold. On July 26, 1856, at the age of 29, he married Lydie Elodie Marie Victoire Lambert. She was 17 years old, having been born on Mar. 29, 1839, in Laon, Aisne, France (northeast of Paris). On the marriage certificate he is registered as George Pierre Arnold Boscowitz. The couple had four (or perhaps five children). The first was Marthe Arnoldine Lydie who was born on June 21, 1857 in Neuilly-sur-Seine. She died on Mar. 22, 1879 in Colombes at the age of 21. Neuilly and Colombes are in or very near Paris. The next child was also a daughter. Her name was Louise Charlotte Hortense. She was born on Oct 5, 1858 in Civray, Cher (about 250 km south of Paris). We have no further information about her. Their only son Pierre Arnold Boscowitz was born on June 18, 1866 in Paris. He was tragically killed in a train accident on August 3, 1884 at the age of 18 in Colombes, see here. He is buried in Colombes together on August 3, 1884 at the age of 18 in Colombes, see here. Another daughter, also named Marthe Boscowitz, was born on Nov. 2, 1879 in Colombes. She lived to adulthood, married and had four children. She died on Oct. 10, 1955 in Pompaples in the Canton of Vaud in Switzerland. (There may have been another daughter named Madelaine. We do not know.)

    George Pierre Arnold was a rentier (a man of independent means) and became an homme des lettres (an educated, scholarly person who makes his living, if needed, through literary and intellectual pursuits). He wrote under the name of Arnold Boscowitz. (His father Aron Loeb was sometimes known as Arnold.) He published at least two books both of which were highly praised. The first, titled Les Volcans et les tremblements de terre was first published in 1866. A later edition (the name also went through some changes) has a photo of himself and his son (who died in 1884) and a touching dedication to his son, see here and here. The book is available on-line, as is its English translation by C. B. Pitman titled Earthquakes first published in 1890 by the publishing house George Routledge and Sons, London. It was reissued in paperback (in French) in October of 2023. In 1886 Arnold Boscowitz was awarded the Prix Marcelin Guérin from the Académie Française for this book. The second book titled L'Âme de la Plante (The Soul of the Plant) was published in 1867. It was reissued in English in December 8, 2013 by Nabu Press.

    George Pierre Arnold died on Sept. 18, 1909. In an obituary that appeared the next day in the newspaper Le Temps, it was written: We regret to learn of the death in Paris of Mr. Arnold Boscowitz, one of our longest-serving and earliest contributors. Among the many newspapers and magazines to which he contributed during his long career, we will mention along with Le Temps, La République Française, La Paix, and La Revue Germanique. He was the author of Volcanoes, Earthquakes, a work awarded a prize by the French Academy, and The Soul of the Plant. In France, this small book, of rare elegance of style and of a very particular originality, has been frequently imitated; in England, it inspired Charles Darwin's work on carnivorous plants. The great naturalist was pleased to acknowledge this, and he held The Soul of the Plant in high esteem. Mr. Arnold Boscowitz was eighty-two years old. The burial will take place in Colombes in the strictest privacy.

    The newspaper Le Temps was one of Paris's most important daily newspapers from April 1861 to November 1942. It was a serious paper of record. After the war the newspaper Le Monde was founded to replace Le Temps as the newspaper of record.

    Even The Athenaeum, a London newspaper, published an obituary on Arnold Boscowitz on Sep 25 1909. In it it says: M. Arnold Boscowitz, author and journalist, dies at age 82 in Colombes, France; his works influenced Darwin's monograph on insectivorous plants and also mentions his two books.

    The living descendants of Aron Loeb and George Pierre Arnold are via the latter's daughter Marthe Boscowitz born in Colombes on Nov. 2, 1879. On Oct. 6, 1898 (at the age of almost 19) she married Elie Albert Kohler (1873-1933) in Paris. He was from Switzerland. He was a Pastor. The couple had four children. Arnold Albert Kohler (1899-1991) born in Leme, France (near the Belgium border) where Elie Albert was a Pastor in the commune, Etiennette Kohler (1904-1921) born in Paris, Marthe Lydie Kohler (1913-1987), called Bobette, born in Geneva, and Paul Kohler (1917-2003) also born in Geneva. Elie Albert Kohler graduated in theology and German literature in Paris. From about 1900 the family lived in Paris, then in Saint-Pargoire in the south of France, later in Geneva, and then in Hamburg where Elie Albert Kohler had a university position. It was here that their daughter Etienette died at the age of 17 of tuberculosis. The family finally moved to Savagnier, a little village in the canton of Neuchâtel, Switzerland. Elie Albert died there in 1933. His wife Marthe, nee Boscowitz, did not appreciate living in a peasant village after her childhood among the intellectual class of Paris. As mentioned above, Marthe died in 1955 in Switzerland. Her death was also due to tuberculosis.
